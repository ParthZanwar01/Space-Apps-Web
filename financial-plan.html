ORCA Financial Plan

A comprehensive financial roadmap for transforming orbital debris into sustainable infrastructure

Three-Phase Financial Strategy

ORCA will be developed and rolled out in three major phases, with a clear roadmap of technical milestones, funding needs, and business growth at each stage. Each phase has specific goals and go/no-go checkpoints to manage risk and demonstrate viability before moving to the next stage. In Phase 1, we prove the core technology, in Phase 2, we prove the business on a limited scale, and in Phase 3, we scale up to full operations.

Phase 1: Proof-of-Concept (2025–2029)

Estimated Cost

$5–10 Million

Duration

4 years (2025-2029)

Financial Status

Operating at a loss (normal for startup R&D phase)

Key Financial Activities

1

R&D Investment

Most expenses are R&D including salaries, prototype development, and lab demonstrations of debris capture technologies.

2

Microgravity Experiments

Funding for parabolic flights or small payloads on the ISS to test melting and printing processes in zero-gravity.

3

CubeSat Demo Mission

Launch costs for small "seed factory" CubeSat to validate core technology with orbital demonstration by 2028.

Primary Funding Sources

Research Grants

NASA SBIR grants, ESA technology development programs

Seed Investments

Early-stage investor funding for concept validation

Demo Contracts

Small agency contracts for demonstration missions

Phase 2: ORCA Pilot Hub Deployment (2030–2035)

Estimated Cost

$100–250 Million

Duration

5 years (2030-2035)

Financial Status

Major capital expenditures with initial revenues, targeting break-even by 2033-2035

Financial Milestones

1

Capital Expenditures

Major costs for building the ORCA hub, tug satellites, and fleet of micro-debris drones, plus launch costs.

2

Initial Revenue Generation

Start seeing initial revenues in millions per year from debris removal contracts and printing jobs for clients.

3

Break-Even Achievement

Annual revenues reach tens of millions, roughly matching operating costs by end of Phase 2.

4

Recurring Revenue

Begin generating recurring revenue from debris removal fees and in-orbit fabrication services.

Funding Sources

Government Contracts

NASA and DoD debris removal missions, ESA Zero Debris initiatives

Venture Capital

Series A/B funding rounds for major infrastructure investment

Strategic Partnerships

Corporate partners investing in or co-funding the project

Phase 3: Scaled Operations & Expansion (2036–2045)

Estimated Cost

$300–500 Million

Duration

9 years (2036-2045)

Financial Status

Robust profitability with significant cash flow and self-sustaining operations

Revenue Scaling

1

Revenue Curve Steepening

Revenues scale up to hundreds of millions as ORCA becomes an orbital services provider at scale.

2

New Revenue Models

Introduction of subscription plans and joint ventures with commercial space station operators.

3

Cost Efficiency

Costs grow more slowly thanks to efficiencies and reusing materials instead of buying new ones.

4

Self-Sustaining Growth

Growth funded by reinvesting ORCA's own profits rather than external funding.

Major Financial Events

IPO Preparation

Taking the company public or large-scale financing rounds for expansion

Lunar Expansion

Establishing presence in lunar orbit funded by significant cash flow

Joint Ventures

Partnerships with big industry players for co-location and revenue sharing

Financial Projections Overview

Based on this phased approach, ORCA's financial plan shows an initial period of heavy investment and negative cash flow (during Phase 1 and Phase 2 when we are doing R&D and building everything) followed by breakeven and profitability by mid-Phase 2.

Break-Even Timeline

1

Phase 1: R&D Investment

Most expenses are R&D (salaries, prototype development) with little to no revenue aside from grants. Normal startup operating loss.

2

Phase 2: Capital & Initial Revenue

Major capital expenditures for hub construction and launches, but initial revenues in millions per year from contracts. Target break-even by 2033-2035.

3

Phase 3: Scale & Profitability

Revenues scale to hundreds of millions as ORCA becomes an orbital services provider, with costs growing more slowly due to efficiencies.

Profitability Trajectory

Net Margin Improvement

Significant improvement in Phase 3 due to operational efficiencies and material reuse

Self-Sustaining Enterprise

ORCA positioned as financially self-sustaining by the 2040s

Strong ROI

Capital-intensive start but strong return on investment once orbital recycling factories are operational

Unit Economics

ORCA's service is compelling on a per-unit economic basis. Producing material in orbit via ORCA is estimated to cost about $2,000 per kg, whereas launching material from Earth costs around $10,000 per kg.

ORCA Production Cost

$2,000 per kg in orbit

Earth Launch Cost

$10,000 per kg from Earth

Cost Advantage

5× cost advantage over Earth launches

Customer Pricing

$5,000 per kg (50% savings for customers)

ORCA Gross Margin

~60% gross margin on services

Economic Advantages

Dual Revenue Stream

Monetize debris twice: get paid to remove it, then sell the recycled product made from that debris

Collision Prevention Value

Preventing a single $200 million satellite collision more than justifies ORCA's services

Economies of Scale

More debris processed means lower average cost per kg as ORCA scales up

Value Proposition

1

Cost Savings

Customers save 50% on material costs compared to Earth launches while ORCA maintains healthy margins

2

Risk Mitigation

Preventing satellite collisions provides significant value beyond just material cost savings

3

Sustainable Operations

Transforming costly waste problem into ongoing revenue-generating operation

Financial Summary

Overall, our financial plan shows a capital-intensive start but a strong return on investment once ORCA's orbital recycling factories are up and running. By transforming a costly waste problem into an ongoing revenue-generating operation, ORCA aims to achieve both its mission impact and solid profitability on a timeline that matches the growth of the new space economy.

Mission Impact

Cleaning up orbital debris while creating sustainable infrastructure

Solid Profitability

Strong financial returns through innovative business model

Market Timing

Timeline aligned with growth of the new space economy
